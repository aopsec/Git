With the course objectives established, we now turn to the core of the evaluation process: **Understanding the task purpose and workflow**.

This foundational logic ensures your ratings accurately reflect the real-world Google Maps user experience.


## 

**Task Purpose**

This project evaluates the "**Full Personalization**" feature on Google Maps to determine how effectively it customizes search results for individual users.

To evaluate this feature, you will:

- **Submit** both assigned and custom search queries.
    
- **Record** the Google Maps responses using screenshots.
    
- **Answer** evaluation questions to rate the quality of the responses.
    

**Note:**

**Project details change often.** You may be asked to compare "Full Personalization" results against "Non-Personalized" results, or you may be asked to rate personalized results on their own.

Because project requirements can shift from one week to the next, you are expected to **review the provided guidelines** (_PDF found at the end of the course_) **every time a new project is assigned.**

# 

Lesson 2: Helpfulness Rating

When assigning a Helpfulness rating for a Personalization task, evaluate whether the Maps results are directly relevant to you as the specific user.

## 

**The Evaluation Mindset**

To ensure accuracy, keep the following mindset in mind:

If a task doesn't apply to you, you can change it slightly to be relevant. For instance, if a question asks you about your trip to another country, you can change that to be about your trip to another state or region. Please do answer every question, though, even if it's not directly relevant to you.

- **The Scale**: You will always use a dropdown scale ranging from 1 (Not helpful at all) to 5 (Extremely helpful).
    

### 

**Rating Guidance**

|If the result is...|Then consider...|
|---|---|
|**Irrelevant to your intent**|A lower rating (1-2)|
|**Accurate but generic**|A mid-range rating (3)|
|**Perfectly tailored to your needs**|A high rating (4-5)|

Helpfulness Rating Scale

1- Not Helpful at All

2 - Somewhat Helpful

3 - Mostly Helpful

4 - Very Helpful

5 - Extremely Helpful

We will now review each of the rating options available to you.

Before we examine the specific levels of the scale, please keep this critical requirement in mind: **Always refer to the Weekly Guidelines Table.**

Because the project scope can shift from one week to the next, you must rely on the **current weekly rating table** to determine exactly which rating to select.

**Do not rely on memory from previous tasks, as the criteria for each choice may have been updated.**

Helpfulness Rating Scale

1- Not Helpful at All

2 - Somewhat Helpful

3 - Mostly Helpful

4 - Very Helpful

5 - Extremely Helpful

A not at all helpful response has one or more of the following characteristics:

- Fails to satisfy primary user intent or query requirement.
    
- Contains significant issues.
    
- Is clearly inappropriate or difficult to understand.
    
- Has unnatural language, content, or structure to the point of being useless.
    

Example:

- **Query**: "Best pizza in Chicago, Illinois"
    
- **Maps Result**: A listing for a "Pizza Hut" located in East Chicago, Indiana.
    
- **Feedback**: This fails to satisfy the primary user intent. The user is looking for local recommendations in a specific city (Chicago), and the result provides a generic chain in a different location. It is essentially useless for the user’s current context.

Helpfulness Rating Scale

1- Not Helpful at All

2 - Somewhat Helpful

3 - Mostly Helpful

4 - Very Helpful

5 - Extremely Helpful

A somewhat helpful response has one or more of the following characteristics:

- Only somewhat addresses the primary user intent.
    
- Contains a significant amount of extra, irrelevant information.
    
- Lacks useful details or specifics.
    
- It may be somewhat difficult to understand.
    
- May have minor inaccuracies or be slightly inappropriate.
    

Example:

- **Query**: "Emergency vet open now"
    
- **Maps Results**: A list of five local veterinary clinics, but three of them are marked "Closed" and one doesn't have a phone number listed.
    
- **Feedback**: It somewhat addresses the intent by providing vets in the area, but it lacks the critical detail of being "open now." The user has to do extra work to filter through closed businesses during an emergency.



1- Not Helpful at All

2 - Somewhat Helpful

3 - Mostly Helpful

4 - Very Helpful

5 - Extremely Helpful

A mostly helpful response has the following characteristics:

- Addresses the primary user intent.
    
- Has a structure that makes it easy to understand.
    
- May include some unnecessary detail or inaccuracies.
    
- Is expressed in a natural way, comparable to an average human response.
    

Example:

- **Query**: "Coffee shops with Wi-Fi nearby"
    
- **Result**: A list of three local cafes with their addresses and star ratings. The description for one mentions "Good for working."
    
- **Feedback**: This addresses the primary intent and is easy to understand. However, it’s not "very" helpful because it doesn't explicitly confirm the Wi-Fi status for all listings or show current busyness levels, requiring the user to click around or guess.
1- Not Helpful at All

2 - Somewhat Helpful

3 - Mostly Helpful

4 - Very Helpful

5 - Extremely Helpful

A very helpful response has the following characteristics:

- Addresses the primary user intent well.
    
- Has a structure that makes it very easy to understand and easy to identify the main points and leads with the most important information.
    
- Is expressed in a natural way, comparable to a talented/well-informed human response.
    

Example:

- **Query**: "Gas stations on my route to Denver"
    
- **Result**: A list of gas stations directly along the plotted navigation route, sorted by the shortest detour time. It includes current gas prices and highlights which ones have clean restrooms.
    
- **Feedback**: This leads with the most important information (price and time added to trip). It is expressed naturally and helps the user make a quick, well-informed decision without leaving the navigation screen.


1- Not Helpful at All

2 - Somewhat Helpful

3 - Mostly Helpful

4 - Very Helpful

5 - Extremely Helpful

An extremely helpful response has the following characteristics:

- Addresses the primary user intent exceptionally well.
    
- Expresses the same content as a ‘Very helpful’ response but in a more fluent, scannable manner.
    
- Has an optimal structure and types of content for satisfying the primary user intent.
    
- Leads with the most important information.
    
- No repetitive or unnecessary information/content.
    
- Hard to imagine a better response.
    

Example:

- **Query**: "Outdoor dining with vegetarian options in Austin"
    
- **Result**: A concise list of top-rated spots that explicitly tags "Outdoor Seating" and "Vegetarian Friendly." The top result shows a photo of the patio, an approximate wait time, a link to the vegetarian menu section, and a button to "Book a Table" immediately.
    
- **Feedback**: This is the optimal structure. It anticipates every need—ambiance (photo), food requirements (menu link), and urgency (wait time/booking). It is perfectly scannable and satisfies the intent so well that the user doesn't need to perform a second search.




### 

**Critical Evaluation Questions**

To help you align your evaluation with the correct rating, use the following guiding questions as you review each result:

- **Relevance to Query**: How directly does the Maps result relate to the specific search terms I entered?
    
- **Visual Evidence**: What exact elements am I seeing in the results that should raise or lower my rating?
    
- **How Personalized is it?**
    

Let's take a look at how the following considerations appear in real tasks.

### 

Example: Identifying a Mismatch

Consider a search for **[Bookstores open on Sunday]**:

|If the Map Result shows...|The Impact on your Rating|
|---|---|
|Bookstores that are **closed** on Sunday|**Major Negative:** The result fails the explicit constraint of the query.|
|Bookstores that are **open** but 50 miles away|**Moderate Negative**: The result meets the "open" criteria but ignores the implied "near me" intent.|
|Bookstores that are **open** and nearby|**Positive**: The result successfully meets both explicit and implied needs.|

## 

**Writing Purposeful Comments**

After assigning a Helpfulness rating to a query, you must provide a clear explanation for your choice. A "purposeful" comment goes beyond simply stating the score; it justifies it using specific evidence.

### 

**Elements of a Strong Comment**

To write a comment that meets the project's quality standards, take these four elements into account:

- **Relation to the Query**: Explain how well the results addressed your specific search intent.
    
- **The Maps Result**: Describe the actual content or businesses provided by Google Maps in the response.
    
- **Specific Map Elements**: Point to exact details or features (e.g., categories, distance, business hours, or reviews) that justified your rating.
    
- **Guideline Alignment**: Explicitly connect your reasoning to the criteria found in the Weekly Helpfulness Rating Table.
    

### 

**What to Avoid**

Do **not** use generic "stock" answers. These provide no value to the evaluation process.

- **Bad Example**: "The suggestions were helpful."
    
- **Good Example**: "The query asked for 'Vegan bakeries open now.' While the map provided three bakeries, two are currently closed according to their business hours, and one is a traditional deli with no vegan options listed. This fails both the explicit 'open' constraint and the 'vegan' category intent."
    

**Pro Tip:** It is highly recommended that you review the Example Tasks provided in the guidelines. These serve as a benchmark for the precise level of detail the client expects in your evaluations.

### 

**Identifying High-Quality Comments**

To master the art of rationale writing, practice sorting comments into "Good" and "Bad" buckets. This helps you recognize the level of precision the client requires.

Drag each comment into the most appropriate type: Good or Bad.

# 

Lesson 3: Side-by-Side Rating

## 

**Introduction to Side-by-Side Rating**

In some instances, the guidelines will require you to perform a **Side-by-Side (SxS) comparison**. This involves evaluating a "**Non-Full Personalization**" result against a "**Full Personalization**" result for the same query.

### 

**The Selection Process**

For each comparison, you will choose one of the following three options from the dropdown menu:

- **Personalization**: The personalized results were superior.
    
- **Non-Personalization**: The generic results were superior.
    
- **Both Were About the Same**: There was no significant difference in quality or relevance.
    

### 

**Core Evaluation Questions**

The specific criteria for your comparison may change weekly. You will be asked to base your rating on a specific question, such as:

- _"Which side had better descriptions?"_
    
- _"Which side was more helpful to the query?"_
    

### 

**Best Practice: Documentation**

While the rating is a dropdown selection, it is a **best practice** to leave a detailed explanation in the **Comment Column** of your feedback sheet. This helps the quality team understand the logic behind your preference.

Lesson 4: Reporting Issues

## 

**Introduction to Issues Flags**

As part of the evaluation task, you are responsible for flagging any errors or inconsistencies present in Maps' response to a query.

As covered earlier in the "Task Anatomy," there are three specific columns dedicated to this process:

- •
    
    **Issues**: Use this column to select specific flags for problems you identify. Because definitions may change, always **click the link in the column header** to ensure your selection aligns with the current criteria.
    
- •
    
    **Explanation for each selected issue**: For every flag you apply, you must provide a written justification explaining exactly why it was marked as an issue.
    
- •
    
    **Other Issues**: Use this space to document any relevant problems you encounter that are not covered by the official dropdown list in the "Issues" column.
    

### 

**Understanding the Official Flags**

We will now examine each official flag in detail to ensure you can identify them accurately during your task.

Click through each "+" button to learn more about each flag.



## 

1. Latency (> 20 sec)

**Definition**: It takes longer than 20 seconds for the system to deliver a first response.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Find a coffee shop near me with a quiet atmosphere."
    
- **Maps Result**: [Loading spinner persists for 25 seconds before showing a list of 3 cafes].
    
- **Explanation**: The delay exceeds the 20-second threshold, causing a poor user experience and potential abandonment.
    

## 

2. Bad Introduction

**Definition**: The introductory text is awkward, overly verbose, or provides no value to the user.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Find a quiet park nearby to read a book."
    
- **Maps Result**: "Central Park, 4.8 Stars, 0.2 miles away. A large green space with many benches."
    
- **Explanation**: The introduction is "bad" because it is overly clinical and provides no added value.
    

## 

3. Too Much Focus on Cuisine

**Definition**: The response prioritizes food type personalization while ignoring specific environmental or service-based requests.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Quiet Italian place with outdoor seating for a business meeting."
    
- **Maps Result**: A list of 3 high-energy, loud Italian delis with no outdoor seating.
    
- **Explanation**: The system hyper-focused on "Italian" but ignored the "quiet" and "outdoor seating" requirements, which were more important for a business meeting context.
    

## 

4. Robotic/Awkward Personalization

**Definition**: Mentions of user preference feel clunky or "uncanny valley" rather than helpful advice.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Suggest a weekend lunch spot."
    
- **Maps Result**: "Because your profile indicates a 74% affinity for 'Tacos' based on your Tuesday history, you should visit Taco Bell." With a Map result to the nearest Taco Bell.
    
- **Explanation**: While personalized, the phrasing is technical and stiff. A better version would be, "Since you seem to enjoy tacos, you might like Taco Bell."
    

## 

5. Bad Place Suggestions

**Definition**: The selected places offered in the response are not a good fit for the user who issued the query i.e., selected places aren't places the user would actually consider visiting.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Luxury hotels in NYC."
    
- **Maps Result**: A list of budget hostels and 2-star roadside motels in New Jersey.
    
- **Explanation**: A user searching for luxury hotels will not consider budget hostels.
    

## 

6. Ignored Instructions or Query Content

**Definition**: Explicit constraints (e.g., "open now," "under $20") are bypassed.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Vegan restaurants open after 10 PM."
    
- **Maps Result**: A top-rated vegan bistro that closes at 8 PM.
    
- **Explanation**: The system ignored the time constraint, making the suggestion useless for a user looking for food right now.
    

## 

7. Missed Personalization Opportunity

**Definition**: The response is generic despite the system having clear data that could have improved the recommendation.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Gyms nearby."
    
- **Maps Result**: A standard list of the 3 closest gyms.
    
- **Explanation**: If the user is a member of "OrangeTheory" and there is one nearby, the system should have highlighted it or prioritized it based on their known membership/history.
    

## 

8. Wrong Assumption

**Definition**: Recommendations are based on a "weak signal" that doesn't actually reflect the user's preferences.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Where should I get dinner tonight?"
    
- **Maps Result**: "Since you love Gluten-Free bakeries, here is a GF pizza place."
    
- **Explanation**: The user only searched for a GF bakery once for a friend's birthday. The result wrongly assumed the user only eats Gluten-Free based on that single interaction.
    

## 

9. Creepy or Intrusive Tone

**Definition**: The response refers to personal data in a way that feels like a privacy violation.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Where should I go for a walk?"
    
- **Maps Result**: "I noticed you spent 3 hours at your ex-girlfriend’s house yesterday; perhaps you'd like a walk in the park nearby to clear your head?"
    
- **Explanation**: This is highly intrusive and uses sensitive location history in a socially inappropriate way.
    

## 

10. Ignored Personal Relationship to a Place

**Definition**: The system treats a "frequented" or "saved" place as if they are new to the user.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Best sushi in my neighborhood."
    
- **Maps Result**: "You might want to try 'Sushi Zen.' It has 4.5 stars and serves fresh fish."
    
- **Explanation**: The user has "Sushi Zen" saved as "Favorite" and has checked in there 20 times. The response should say, "Your favorite spot, Sushi Zen, is still the best-rated nearby."
    

## 

11. Missing Information

**Definition**: The response leaves out a "deal-breaker" detail requested or implied.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Hike with a great view that is dog-friendly."
    
- **Maps Result**: "Try the Sunset Trail; it has the best views of the valley."
    
- **Explanation**: The result fails to mention if dogs are allowed, which was a primary requirement of the query.
    

## 

12. Bad Formatting & Other Usability Issues

**Definition**: The response is badly formatted or has other usability issues like a non-functional link.

**Example**:

- **Query**: "Top 3 sights in Rome."
    
- **Maps Result**: 1.Colosseum2.VaticanMuseums3.Pantheon (All text bunched together without spaces or bullets).
    
- **Explanation**: The lack of spacing or bullet points makes the list difficult to scan and use.
